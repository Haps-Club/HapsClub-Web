## What Haps Club is

Haps Club is a Los Angeles recommendations operation. It exists in three coordinated surfaces:

1. **The website** — `https://haps.club/` — a daily LA picks homepage, **updated every day**. The featured pick reflects what's worth doing *today*. Lives as one file (`index.html`) in the `Hilex2030/haps-club` repo.
2. **The newsletter** — Haps Weekly, sent **every Tuesday** via Kit, to ~1,200+ readers. A weekly preview of the most worth-going-to events of the coming week. Goes out before the week starts so readers can plan.
3. **The events** — Haps Club hosts its own real-world meetups in LA, ticketed via Luma, promoted in the newsletter with the coupon code `HAPSCLUBNEWSLETTER`.

The three surfaces feed each other but play different roles:

- **The newsletter is the weekly preview.** Once a week, it tells readers what's coming. Readers open it Tuesday morning and decide what they're doing this week.
- **The site is the live daily reference.** Readers come back through the week to check today's pick, see what's still happening this weekend, and pull up venue info on the fly.
- **The events are the in-person product.** When Haps Club is hosting something that week, it leads the newsletter and lives at the top of the site.

The Tuesday newsletter and the site share the same week's picks but frame them differently. The newsletter says *"here's what's coming."* The site says *"here's what's on today."*

## Who it's for

- Adults, roughly **30–55**
- Mostly **single, but never described as "singles"** — the framing is always community, getting out, doing things, not "for single people"
- **LA residents**, with a Westside bias (Venice, Santa Monica, WeHo, Brentwood, Culver City, Mar Vista) but reaches across the city — Highland Park, Echo Park, Boyle Heights, DTLA, etc., are all in scope
- People who **read** — books, magazines, longform — and who'd rather hear about a film series than a club bottle service
- People who already live full lives and use Haps Club to find the *good* version of a Tuesday night, not to fill empty time
- People who want to **do something and be around other people doing it** — not stay home, not eat dinner alone

## What Haps Club does for them

It tells them what's worth showing up to this week. It pushes them out of the house, into rooms with other people, and toward the things in LA that reward effort. It celebrates the city across its full range — the big institutions and the small new things — through one specific person's taste.

## Who it's not for

- Tourists looking for "things to do in LA"
- 22-year-olds bar-crawling
- Listicle audiences ("17 hidden gems")
- Anyone who'd respond to "Don't miss this!" exclamation-mark copy

## Voice — the single most important thing

The voice is **a real person who lives in LA and reads books**, not a startup writing copy and not an algorithm summarizing Eater.

### What that sounds like

- Conversational, declarative, specific: *"Cinespia is screening Mulholland Drive Saturday at Hollywood Forever. Doors 7:15, film at 8:30."*
- Always names the **neighborhood**: Highland Park, Echo Park, Atwater Village, etc.
- Times use natural language: *"Doors 7:15, film 8:30"* — not *"7:15 PM doors."*
- Em dashes — for parentheticals — are fine. Don't stack two in one sentence.
- One specific detail beats three generic adjectives. *"Modern French in Pacific Palisades, Art Deco room"* > *"upscale, romantic, beautiful French restaurant."*
- **Picks teach the move, not the destination.** Don't just point at a thing — tell readers the specific sequence that makes it good. ✅ *"Pick up a burrito from Lily's in Malibu, drive ten more minutes north, walk down the cliff stairs at El Matador, eat while the sea stacks turn gold. Before noon for parking."* / ❌ *"El Matador Beach is beautiful — go check it out."*

### What it never sounds like

- Never promotional: ❌ *"Don't miss this!"* / ❌ *"You won't want to miss…"* / ❌ *"Hottest spot in LA"*
- **Never uses exclamation marks in card bodies or story copy.** They cheapen everything.
- Never uses these words: *cancellation, monetize, democratize, streamline, unleash, leverage, ecosystem, journey, curated, hidden gem, must-visit, hot spot, vibe-y, slay, iconic (about food), elevated (about anything)*
- Never says **"singles"** about the audience
- Never describes things in a way that sounds like marketing copy. If a sentence could appear on a brand's About page, rewrite it.

### Tone calibration

Imagine writing to one specific friend: someone smart, busy, who lives in LA, who's already seen everything once, who wants the actual good version. Not the friend who needs convincing.

## Editorial center of gravity

**Haps Club is about doing, not consuming. About gathering, not browsing.**

The strongest picks are **activities that bring people into the same room**: film screenings, run clubs, gallery openings, book talks, classes, pop-ups, ticketed events, group hikes, live music, art shows, public lectures, supper clubs, screenings, races, exhibitions on opening weekend. Anything where a reader who shows up will find other humans doing the same thing.

Restaurants and bars belong in the mix, but they're the **supporting cast**, not the lead. A great restaurant earns a spot in Restaurant Picks; a great event earns the lead. If a week has six worthy events and three worthy restaurants, the issue tilts toward the events. Haps Club is not a dining guide.

### Range across scale — this is the editorial signal

A good week's mix covers **the full range of the city**:

- **Big institutions**: The Broad, LACMA, Hollywood Bowl, Greek Theatre, MOCA, Cinespia at Hollywood Forever, the Music Center. These are part of LA. Cover them when something specific is happening (a new exhibit opening, a programmed night, a one-off concert) — not as evergreen "you should visit" recs.
- **Mid-scale venues and series**: Sofar Sounds, Jazz at LACMA, Vidiots, gallery rows, neighborhood film series, established run clubs.
- **Small and new**: a 40-person zine release in Highland Park, a supper club above a wine bar, a one-night pop-up, a brand-new restaurant in its first month, a community garden volunteer day.

The mix itself is what makes Haps Club feel like LA seen through a real Angeleno's eyes — someone who'll go to The Broad on Saturday and the underground reading on Sunday. **Avoid drifting in either direction**: all-institutions reads like a tourist board, all-tiny-things reads like a Substack about hidden gems.

## What gets featured (decision rules)

A pick earns its place when **at least two** of these are true:

- It's an **activity** — something the reader *does* or *attends*, not just "a place that exists."
- It's **time-bound** — happening this week, this month, in season. A Tuesday-night film series this week beats an evergreen restaurant.
- It has a **specific anchor** — a film, an artist, a chef, an event, a date — not just "a good place."
- It rewards effort — the kind of thing a reader is glad they went to, not just glad they bookmarked.

Bonus weight if:
- It brings people **together** — there's a real chance the reader meets someone or shares the experience with strangers
- It contributes to a balanced **range** that issue (small + big, new + established, Westside + east side)
- The reader probably hasn't seen it on Eater/Infatuation that week
- It's hosted by a real person/place with a website you can link to directly

### What gets cut

- **Anything not verifiable.** If you can't confirm the event date, venue, and link, leave it out. The user has caught fabricated picks before. Never invent.
- **Pure consumption recs masquerading as the lead** — "go to this nice restaurant" is a Restaurant Pick, not a featured story. Featured slots go to events.
- **Evergreen institution recs** with no specific reason to go *this week* — "visit The Broad" is not a pick; "Yoko Ono's *Music of the Mind* opens at The Broad this Saturday" is.
- Listicle filler ("10 best tacos") — Haps Club picks one taco place this week, names the neighborhood, says why
- Anything where the only link is an Instagram profile with no website (rare exceptions for well-known venues)
- Anything you'd link via Eater, Infatuation, or Yelp as the primary source — always link to the venue's own site
- Repeats from the previous week's newsletter (long-runway items like the Beverly Hills Art Show or Yoko Ono at The Broad are prime repeat traps — include once)
- Same venue twice in one issue (Featured > Mark Your Calendar > Out & About > Restaurant Picks if there's a conflict — drop from the lower section)

## How the surfaces fit together

```
Sources (inbox, Instagram scrapes, personal knowledge)
        │
        ▼
[Editorial decisions: today's pick + this week's lineup]
        │
        ├──▶ Website (updated DAILY) at haps.club
        │       Today · The Pick = today's lead
        │       This week = upcoming days
        │       Mark your calendar = longer-runway items
        │
        ├──▶ Newsletter (every TUESDAY, Kit)    ──▶  ~1,200+ inboxes
        │       The weekly preview of what's coming
        │
        └──▶ Haps Club's own event              ──▶  Luma + HAPSCLUBNEWSLETTER coupon
                Leads the Tuesday newsletter when applicable
```

The Tuesday newsletter is the **weekly anchor** — it commits the editorial line for the week ahead. The site then **updates daily** through that week, rotating the featured pick to whatever's on today and shifting the rest of the cards forward. By the next Tuesday newsletter, the site has cycled through six days of features.

## Hard rules (these break the project if violated)

1. **Fact-check every pick before it ships.** Never invent events, dates, venues, prices, or descriptions. If you can't verify it via web search, leave it out and tell the user what you couldn't verify.
2. **Verify every date with Python.** Day-of-week mismatches are the #1 embarrassing error. Use `date(yyyy,m,d).strftime('%A')` for every date. Never trust memory.
3. **Verify every URL is live.** Broken links are the #1 reader complaint. Use `web_fetch` or `web_search`. Remove anything you can't confirm.
4. **No exclamation marks in card or story copy.**
5. **Never use the word "singles."**
6. **No third color, no third font, no light/dark toggle on the site.**
7. **Always preserve Google Analytics** (`G-RKY63MK7ND`) when regenerating the site file.
8. **Always include the Kit footer variables** when generating newsletters: `subscriber.first_name`, `subscriber.email_address`, `address`, `unsubscribe_url`, `subscriber_preferences_url`, `message_content`.
9. **Never inline base64 images in newsletter emails.** Kit strips data URIs on send. Use the hosted Cloudinary URL for the logo.
10. **Don't repeat venues across sections of one issue, or across consecutive weeks** of the newsletter for long-runway items.

## Soft rules (judgment calls)

- When in doubt between two picks, the **more time-specific** one wins.
- When in doubt about voice, **read it aloud**. If it sounds like ad copy, rewrite.
- When in doubt about a pick's quality, **leave it out**. A shorter issue with five strong picks is always better than a longer one padded with mediocre ones.
- When the user disagrees with an editorial choice, **defer**. This is one operator running a personal recommendations operation — their taste is the product.

## Maintenance

This file is the source of truth for the *idea* of Haps Club. Update it when:

- The audience definition shifts
- The voice rules change (a new banned word, a new allowed phrase)
- A new surface gets added (podcast, social channel, second city)

- Tactical site changes (those go in `haps-club-website` skill)
